Cheap Holidays to Greece from the UK (2026 Guide)

Last updated: March 2026

18 min read

Published by Budget Holidays Hub — Expert UK Travel Research.

Planning **cheap holidays to Greece from the UK** requires a more strategic approach in 2026 than destinations like Spain or Turkey. Greece is a archipelago of over 200 inhabited islands, and the price of your holiday is dictated almost entirely by which island you choose and how you get there. While Greece has a reputation for high-end luxury in places like Mykonos or Santorini, it remains one of the most rewarding **budget holidays Greece** for UK travellers who know where to look. From the vast, value-packed island of Crete to the hidden coves of the Peloponnese, **cheap Greece holidays 2026** offer a Mediterranean escape that balances cultural depth with incredible natural beauty.

For most UK travellers, cost expectations for a week in Greece range from £350 for a budget-friendly package in Kos to £800 for a well-located stay in the Cyclades. Greece is ideal for island-hopping adventurers, families seeking traditional "village" vibes, and couples who want the white-and-blue aesthetic without the premium price tag. In this guide, we break down the regional cost differences, flight strategies, and local secrets that ensure you get the maximum value for your pound.

The Great Greek Island Price Divide

Greece pricing varies heavily by island, and understanding the hierarchy of "Island Value" is the first step to a cheap holiday. While cheap holidays to Santorini from the UK are possible off-season, they will always cost more for basic amenities than a holiday in Crete or Rhodes. Santorini often exceeds the cost of a full holiday in Crete on accommodation alone, particularly in the "Caldera view" villages.

Crete remains one of the few Greek destinations where full holidays under £500 are still realistic throughout the spring and autumn. Because of its size, Crete produces much of its own food, meaning the "taverna tax" found on smaller, infertile islands is much lower. For those seeking maximum value, the northern coast of Crete (around Rethymno or Hersonissos) offers the highest density of budget resorts and low-cost flights from UK hubs like Manchester and Birmingham.

Crete: The Budget Champion

Crete is the largest Greek island and the undisputed king of budget travel. Its size means it has a huge range of accommodation, from simple "studios" (the Greek equivalent of a self-catering apartment) to high-end resorts. In 2026, we recommend cheap holidays to Crete from the UK as the primary choice for those who want the full Greek experience on a limited budget. Beyond the beaches, Crete's mountains and gorges are free to explore, providing endless value for outdoor enthusiasts.

Rhodes and Corfu: The Package Hubs

Rhodes and Corfu are both "package friendly" islands. This means they are served by multiple UK airports and have a high volume of hotels that partner with major UK tour operators. This volume drives prices down. Corfu is particularly popular because it has the shortest flight time from the UK (around 3 hours), which often leads to cheaper fares from regional airports like Bristol or Newcastle. Rhodes is famed for its medieval Old Town and stunning Lindos beaches, both of which can be enjoyed on a modest budget if you stay slightly inland.

Flights to Greece: Regional UK Airport Strategy

Flights to Greece are generally more expensive than Spain because the distances are greater and the volume of flights is lower. To find the best deals, you must be flexible with your departure airport.

  • **London Advantage:** London Stansted (STN) and Gatwick (LGW) offer the highest frequency to the broadest range of Greek islands. EasyJet and Ryanair dominate these routes, and if you book 6-10 weeks in advance, return flights for under £150 are common.
  • **The Manchester and Birmingham Powerhouses:** These airports offer exceptional regional UK airport deals to Crete and Rhodes. Jet2 has a massive presence here, and their "package" prices often include the "luxury" of assigned seating and luggage which can be costly "add-ons" on London-based budget routes.
  • **The Scotland Connection:** Flying from Edinburgh or Glasgow to Greece can be expensive. Many Scottish travellers save significant amounts by taking the train or driving to Manchester or Newcastle for their Greek holiday flights.

All-Inclusive vs. Studio Living: The Greek Trade-off

Unlike Turkey, Greece is not "all-inclusive first." While there are many cheap Greece all inclusive holidays, the traditional Greek holiday is built around the "Studio." These are simple, family-run apartments with a small kitchenette.

For budget travellers, "Studio Living" is often the secret to a much cheaper holiday. By shopping at local markets for fresh tomatoes, feta, and bread (which are incredibly cheap and high quality), you can eat breakfast and lunch for under £5. This leaves you with more money to enjoy a high-quality dinner at a local taverna in the evening. However, if you are a large family, the predictable costs of an all-inclusive resort in Rhodes or Kos can still provide better "end-to-end" value.

The 2026 Island Hopping Strategy for Less

Island hopping is the ultimate Greek dream, but it can be a budget-killer if not planned correctly. In 2026, the key to affordable island hopping is focusing on a single "Island Chain" to minimize ferry costs.

  1. **The Saronic Gulf:** Fly to Athens and take ferries to Hydra, Spetses, or Poros. Because these are close to the mainland, the ferries are cheap and frequent.
  2. **The Dodecanese:** Fly to Rhodes and visit Kos, Symi, or Tilos. These islands are close together, meaning ferry times and costs are low.
  3. **The Cyclades (Budget Version):** Avoid Mykonos. Instead, fly to Santorini (for the views) then immediately take a ferry to Naxos or Paros, where the cost of living is 30% lower.

Athens vs. The Islands: A Budget Perspective

While the islands are the main draw for **cheap holidays to Greece from the UK**, starting or ending your trip in Athens can be a masterstroke for budget travellers. Athens has some of the most affordable city-centre accommodation in Europe, provided you look in up-and-coming areas like Koukaki or Kypseli rather than right under the Acropolis. The city is an "open-air museum," and while the major sites like the Parthenon have seen price increases in 2026, many of the best views and atmospheres (like the hills of Filopappou or Lycabettus) are completely free.

The real saving in Athens comes from the food and transport. The Athens Metro is world-class and incredibly cheap, connecting the airport to the city and the port of Piraeus for a fraction of the cost of a private transfer. For dining, the "Laiki Agora" (weekly street markets) offer a glimpse into local life where you can buy a week's worth of incredibly fresh fruit, olives, and local cheese for the price of a single lunch in Mykonos. If your goal is **budget holidays Greece**, don't just use Athens as a transit hub; spend two days here to soak up the culture for less.

Greek Food Culture: Eating Like a Local for Less

In Greece, the concept of "Meze" is your best friend for budgeting. Instead of everyone ordering a large main course, Greek families typically order several small plates to share. This is not only more social but often significantly cheaper. A meal of *Tzatziki*, *Fava* (yellow split pea puree), *Horiatiki* (Greek salad), and some *Kalamarakia* (fried squid) shared between three or four people provides a massive variety of high-quality food for a very low per-person cost.

Always look for the 'Ouzeri' or 'Tsipouradiko'. These are traditional eateries (often found in port towns like Volos or on islands like Crete) where you order a drink (Ouzo or Tsipouro) and get a free small plate of food with it. In many authentic spots, the more drinks you order, the more substantial the food becomes. It is a historic way of eating that remains one of the best **cheap Greece holidays 2026** secrets. Remember: avoid restaurants with pictures of food on the menu or "touts" outside; if you see local Greeks eating there, it’s almost certainly better value and higher quality.

Transportation: Mastering the Aegean for Less

The cost of moving between islands can quickly erode your savings. In 2026, the gap between "Fast Ferries" and "Conventional Ferries" has widened. A high-speed catamaran from Piraeus to Mykonos can cost over €80, while a traditional ferry (like the Blue Star line) might be as low as €35. The slower ferries also allow you to sit on the deck, enjoy the sea breeze, and watch the islands go by—it’s essentially a cruise for the price of a bus ticket.

On the islands themselves, the 'KTEL' bus service is the backbone of budget travel. These buses are clean, efficient, and usually timed to match the arrival of major ferries. In Crete, for example, you can traverse half the island for under €15. Compare this to car rentals, which have surged in price due to insurance and fuel costs. If you do need a car, book it for 2-3 days in the middle of your trip to explore the "wild" interior, rather than renting it for the full week at the airport.

Top 5 Budget Areas to Stay in Greece

  • **Rethymno (Crete):** This Venetian-style town offers a beautiful beach, incredible history, and some of the best value dining in Greece. The local university population keeps the prices in the bars and cafes grounded year-round.
  • **Faliraki (Rhodes):** While it has a "party" reputation, Faliraki has rebranded as a hub for affordable family resorts. It has one of the best beaches on the island and excellent bus links to Rhodes Town.
  • **Sidari (Corfu):** Located in the north of Corfu, Sidari offers stunning rock formations (Canal d'Amour) and a massive range of budget-friendly self-catering studios that are popular with UK holidaymakers.
  • **Nafplio (Peloponnese):** Often called the most beautiful town in Greece, Nafplio is on the mainland but feels like an island. It’s a great base for exploring ancient sites like Epidaurus and Mycenae without the "island premium" on food and hotels.
  • **Tsilivi (Zante/Zakynthos):** A fantastic alternative to the more expensive Laganas. Tsilivi offers a great mix of family-run apartments, shallow safe beaches, and traditional tavernas that haven't inflated their prices for the "Instagram" crowd.

Shopping and Souvenirs: The Budget Traveller's Guide

Greece produces world-class products that make incredible souvenirs, but you shouldn't buy them in the duty-free shop. For olive oil, honey, and herbs, head to the local supermarket or a village cooperative. The "unbranded" honey sold by the side of the road in the mountains of Crete or the Peloponnese is often the best you’ll ever taste and costs a fraction of the bottled version in tourist shops.

If you're looking for leather goods (like the famous Greek sandals), Athens' Psirri district is the place to go. You can find craftsmen making them by hand for €25-€35, whereas the same sandals in Oia (Santorini) will be €60+. Buying local doesn't just save you money; it ensures your **cheap holiday in Greece** directly supports the local economy and traditional crafts that make the country so special.

Budget Tips for Your Greek Escape

  • **Eat at 'Souvladzidika':** These are local grill houses. A filling gyros wrap (pork or chicken with salad and chips wrapped in pita) is the ultimate Greek budget meal, often costing under €5.
  • **Book Ferries in Advance:** Use sites like 'Ferryhopper' to compare prices. Note that the slower "conventional" ferries are often 50% cheaper than the high-speed "catamarans" and provide a more scenic, relaxing journey.
  • **Master the 'KTEL' Bus:** Greece has an excellent regional bus network called KTEL. They are reliable, air-conditioned, and connect all the major ports to the best beaches for just a few Euros. They are much cheaper than the often-expensive island taxis.
  • **House Wine is Your Friend:** Order wine by the "kilo" or "half-kilo" in carafes at tavernas. It's usually the owner's own production (or from a local co-op), it's delicious, and it's much cheaper than bottled wine.
  • **Travel in 'Shoulder' Season:** May and early June, or September and October, are the "sweet spots." The weather is perfect (22-26°C), the sea is warm (especially in autumn), and the prices for studios drop by 40% compared to August.
  • **The 'Free' Beach Rule:** In Greece, most beaches are public. While "Beach Clubs" charge heavily for sunbeds and umbrellas, there is almost always a "free zone" where you can lay your own towel. Buy a cheap umbrella at a local mini-market for €10 and it will pay for itself in one day.
  • **Drink the Tap Water (Where Safe):** In Athens and parts of the mainland, tap water is safe and delicious. On most islands, you’ll need bottled water, but buy it in 6-packs from the supermarket (approx €2) rather than single cold bottles from kiosks (approx €0.50-€1 each).

Frequently Asked Questions

Which Greek island is cheapest?

Crete is generally the cheapest Greek island for UK travellers due to the high volume of flights and massive range of budget accommodation. Rhodes and Kos are also excellent value for those looking for package deals.

Is Greece expensive for UK travellers?

Greece offers a wide range of pricing. While Santorini and Mykonos are expensive, islands like Crete, Corfu, and Rhodes are very affordable for UK travellers. Eating out is generally 20-30% cheaper than in the UK, and local transport is exceptionally good value.

When are Greece holidays cheapest?

The cheapest time to visit is May, June, and October. Prices peak dramatically in July and August when most of Europe takes their summer holidays. Early September is also a great value "hidden" month with peak-summer weather but lower prices.

Is it cheaper to fly or take a ferry in Greece?

For long distances (e.g., Athens to Crete), a budget flight booked in advance is often cheaper and much faster than a ferry. For shorter distances between islands in the same chain, the ferry is always the more cost-effective choice.

Do I need to tip in Greece?

Tipping is not mandatory but highly appreciated. In restaurants, rounding up the bill or leaving a few Euros (approx 5-10%) is standard for good service. Many tavernas will also bring you a free dessert or a shot of 'Raki' at the end of your meal as a gesture of hospitality.
